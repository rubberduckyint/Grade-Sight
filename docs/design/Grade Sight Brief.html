01 · Audiences

Two people. Same product. Different Tuesdays.

The product ships from day one for both. The chrome is one system. The moments are not — and that's where the design has to bend.

PARENT

The worried parent on a weeknight.

38–55 · usually mom · public-school zip · 1–3 kids · phone in hand

She's not a math person. The quiz came home in a backpack with a 72% on it and a few red marks. She doesn't want to "help with homework" — she wants to know what she's looking at. Is this normal? Is he falling behind? Is it carelessness or is something genuinely missing?

She'll photograph the paper on the kitchen counter under bad light. She has 8 minutes before dinner. She isn't going to read instructions. If the product makes her feel dumb, she's gone.

What she's actually buying: relief. A sentence she can read once and feel okay.

TEACHER

The math teacher with a stack of quizzes.

26–58 · 90+ students across 4–5 sections · weeknights and Sunday afternoons · laptop, sometimes iPad

He grades for free, on his own time, because the platform his school pays for produces a number. He needs the patterns. Of the 27 kids who got #4 wrong, are they wrong the same way? Because the answer changes whether he reteaches the lesson Tuesday or pulls four kids aside.

He is allergic to bullshit. He has watched five edtech demos this year and hated all of them. He will trust a tool that respects his time and his judgment. He will instantly distrust anything that calls students "learners" or talks about "growth mindset" without a number behind it.

What he's actually buying: leverage. The next intervention, found before he had to find it himself.

An honest note about "research"

I'm not citing a Pew survey or a "73% of parents say…" stat. The two portraits above are synthesized from what's defensibly true about this audience: secondary-math parental engagement is dominated by mothers in their 40s checking work after school; public-school math teachers carry 90–150 students across sections and grade on personal time; both groups have low patience for product-speak; both have phones, but only one is on a laptop most of the time.

If anything in either portrait conflicts with what you're hearing from real users — say so. They're a starting point, not data.

02 · Moments

When does a person actually open this?

Designs should be tuned to these six moments. Not "the parent journey" or "the teacher journey" abstractly — these specific Tuesday-night and Sunday-afternoon situations.

PARENT · 8:14 PM TUESDAY

"Got home, found this in the backpack."

One quiz. Already graded. She wants a sentence about what to do tomorrow. Optimize: low friction to first answer, mobile-first, warm tone.

PARENT · SATURDAY 9 AM

"He has a unit test Monday."

Pulls up his student page. Wants to see what's been recurring. Optimize: longitudinal pattern view; "what to practice this weekend" suggestion.

TEACHER · SUNDAY 4 PM

"Twenty-seven quizzes from 4th period."

Batch upload. Class-level pattern emerging. Optimize: fast multi-upload, clear progress, class roll-up. (Phase 2 surface; design context only.)

TEACHER · WEEKNIGHT 10 PM

"One kid I'm worried about."

Single-student deep view. Wants the pattern across three weeks. Optimize: /students/[id] timeline, intervention suggestion.

EITHER · UPLOAD MOMENT

"Photograph and submit."

The photo is taken on a phone with the paper sitting on a kitchen counter or a teacher's desk. Imperfect lighting. Optimize: tolerant capture, no friction to "submit and wait."

EITHER · THE REVEAL

"The result is back."

The diagnosis page. The single moment the product earns the next visit. Optimize: aggregate first, pattern second, problem third. Not a list of cards.

03 · Principles

Six rules. Every wireframe answers to these.

If a screen breaks one, it goes back to the bench. If two screens fight, the higher-numbered principle wins.

i

Lead with the sentence.

Every diagnosis surface should answer in one sentence before it answers in twelve. The aggregate insight is the headline; the cards are the supporting paragraphs. A parent who reads only the first sentence should still walk away with the right thing to do.

ii

Wrong is interesting, not shameful.

No red/green right-wrong coloring on student work. Errors get the amber "insight" treatment because they are the product's whole point. Use mark only for genuine destructive states (delete, failed upload).

iii

The phone is the primary surface for parents.

Parent uploads happen on a 375-wide viewport in a kitchen. Every parent-prominent flow is designed for the phone first. Teacher-heavy flows (batch upload, archive) can lead with the laptop and degrade gracefully.

iv

Privacy is a surface, not a footnote.

The trust commitments — never sell, US-only data, 30-day deletion, SDPC NDPA — show up on at least three visible surfaces post-signup, not just the policy page. A parent or teacher should never have to dig to feel safe.

v

Editorial, not edtech.

Source Serif 4 carries the human voice. Inter does the work. The product reads like a thoughtful paperback, not a SaaS dashboard. Avoid: graduation caps, sparkle icons, "AI-powered" anywhere on the surface, gradient hero blobs, "growth mindset" copy.

vi

The teacher is a peer.

The engine is correct most of the time, not all of the time. Every diagnosis the engine produces is editable by the teacher in-place. The product never tells a teacher their judgment is wrong; it offers what it sees and gets out of the way.

04 · Tone

What we say. What we don't.

The product's voice is a thoughtful colleague who has already read the paper. Confident, calm, specific. Never breezy, never solemn.

"Marcus got 14 of 18. Three of the four he missed share the same pattern: he's losing the negative when he distributes. That's a 5-minute conversation, not a tutor."
DO · PARENT-FACING

"Three of these four come from the same place." Specific. Empowering. Implies action without prescribing it.

DON'T

"Your child needs to work on their math skills." Generic, parental, vaguely shaming.

DO · TEACHER-FACING

"4th period is missing #6. Same pattern, twenty-three of twenty-seven." Quantified. Class-aware. Speaks his language.

DON'T

"Time to celebrate growth! 🎉" Anything emoji. Anything that calls students "learners." Anything ending in an exclamation.

DO · ON ERROR

"The image was blurry — try again, paper flat under good light." Specific instruction, no apology theater.

DON'T

"Oops! Something went wrong. 😔" No "Oops." No "Whoops." No emoji. Ever.

05 · Limits

What this brief is not.

  • Not a usability study. Nothing here is tested against real parents and teachers. It's a structured guess, calibrated to what's defensibly known. Anything that conflicts with your actual user calls beats anything in this document.
  • Not implementation-bound. Phase 2 and 3 surfaces (cohort pulse, admin, LMS, district views) are sketched only where they help frame the v1 work. We won't wireframe them in this pass.
  • Not a content spec. Real headline copy and microcopy will land in the wireframes themselves and be refined when content lands. The strings here are placeholders that demonstrate tone.
  • Not the design system. The Foundation and Handoff documents from the previous pass remain canonical for tokens, type, components. This brief sits on top.
